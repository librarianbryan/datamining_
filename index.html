DATA MINING
What is it?

Internet tracking. Between April 30 and August 17, I visited 401 sites and 1053 third party sites. Notice the third party sites. That's nearly two and half times the sites I actually saw in my browser window. One study found 55% of website load time was loading third party trackers.
Thanks to EU's GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) we know that Spotify collects every click, what headphones you use, window size, what files you dropped and dropped. It is not a music playing machine. It is a recording machine. This is the desktop app. If we consider the same data collection on via phone app, it also knows where you are at the times, when you sleep, if your moving fast and slow, etc. Your phone is literally an extention of yourself and we are all, no joke, cyborgs. That is a different talk.
Facebook is always watching you. Even when you are not on Facebook. Non-PII can be reverse engineered via the mosaic effect. Academic studies have shown this for years. If this wasn't the case, Facebook would have no reason to track you when you don't use their site.
What's bad about it?

What's bad about it?

You get a bunch of crappy ads. These are annoying and distracting, but if you walk near a hotdog stand you want a notification that hotdogs are on sale right?. Sure. What if the same scenario was framed as "yes, I will give away my Constitutional 4th amend rights, the rights that made the U.S. the envy of world, to see an ad for hotdogs." What if it was framed as a total 180 of the ethical values that seperated us from Soviet bloc totalritarianism, so you can get ads about about caulking if you watching a video about how to retile your bathroom? This what "personalization" means: total surveillence so you can get ads.
Most malware comes from ads. More sinister are phishing attacks that lead to data breaches or doxing. Hey give us your email? Everytime you do this you expose yourself to more phishing attempts. Types harm that can come from privacy violations: identity theft, medical identity theft, doxing (blackmail, sextortion, swatting), stalking / partner abuse. Which is to say nothing of the qualitive costs: social isolation, emotional costs.
malware / phishing / doxing
Nation-state scale psyops; e.g., targeted disinformation campaigns to manipulate elections. My mother is a white, working class voter with a relatively low education level in western PA. She was real hot commodity during the last presidential election. More about my mother later, but I should note Cambridge Analytica got their data in a clandestine manner, and we know about it because of a whistle blower.
Reverse redlining. This when you deliberately target people in geographic region. The most notorious example was during the subprime mortage crisis when bad loans were heavily pushed at particular neighborhoods. Mosiac effect can be used to reverse engineer non-PII. You can learn someone's sex, gender, race, political affiliation, income, health info. This can lead to employment, housing, and education discrimination directly or indirectly. In short, all reasons we don't record what people read.

to do with libraries?

Perhaps these would be better called "public nets." People use our internet. If your software is not configured properly your computers are just fly traps for patron info. It is often the most vulernable that use our computers. This is a double whammy.
more public nets
So we often utilize "free" services, and they are free just because they are fly traps for info.
I am going to give a few quick suggestions of varying scope and difficulty. This in no means comprehensive. Only collect what you absolutely need, make it as anonymous as possible, and protect it righteously when it can't be anonymousized.

What can we do about it?

Put clauses in the vendor contracts with strict rules about patron data even non-PII. Here is an example from Seattle Public Library.
gnarly vendor contracts
Install Tor Browser and your patrons won't be tracked on the internet period. Tor is open source free software. A new version of Tor Browser just got released and it is great.
install Tor Browser
install Privacy Badger
The internet is a surveillence machine and Tor is a monkey wrench in that machine so some websites just won't work in Tor, in which case you can add some plug-ins to Firefox or Chrome. 1) Privacy Badger will block tracking cookies between websites. It also has granular controls so if sights require some cookies, you can turn those on selectively. I'm trying to keep down the number of slides so I just wore this shirt. Set the default search engine in your browser to be DuckDuckGo. This keeps searches anonymous.
I love and respect my mom. My mother is senior citizen with a highschool education. She uses a hand-me-down Android tablet that no longer gets security updates. She does not deserve annoying ads, malware, identity theft, fake news, or a loan with really bad terms. My mother has a human right to not be surveilled. You love and respect your patrons, and your communities. Patrons trust us. If we don't protect patrons privacy, we violate that trust. We have a professional and ethical obligation to mitigate risk to our patrons. When someone says privacy is dead ask them for their password. Now some privacy nihilists are going to say, "The ship has sailed. It's over. Everything is collected." I agree that current state is bad, but does that mean we stop fighting for what is better. This issue is bigger than libraries. If we are heading towards climate disaster does that mean we just shrug our shoulders? A handful of famous men of have fired for sex crimes, does mean we've solved the problem of sexual harassment? Grand juries almost never recommend charges again police officers, does that mean we are going to stop fight for justice in our communities? Of course not, we're librarians. We are not complicit.
